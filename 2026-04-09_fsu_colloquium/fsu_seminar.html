What is computational literacy?

“Computation is not merely a tool for more efficient instruction, but the basis for a new literacy that changes how people think and learn.”
— diSessa (2000)

Like written language or mathematics, computational literacy is a representational medium — a way of constructing, expressing, and communicating ideas.

In disease ecology: You cannot fully understand an SIR model by reading its equations.
You have to build it, run it, break it, and interpret it to understand what it means.

The gap: Most students can execute code they don’t understand
The goal: Move fluently between biological meaning, mathematical formulation, and computational implementation

Computational literacy is distinct from software training or coding bootcamps. It is the ability to think with computation, to use it as a medium for scientific reasoning. In ecology and disease biology, this means being able to translate between what’s happening biologically, how that is formalized mathematically, and how that is implemented computationally.

The core insight from diSessa is that computation changes the kinds of questions you can ask and the ways you can explore them. A student who can only read equations or only execute code is missing the connective tissue that makes computation a tool for discovery.

My research: Individual variation as mechanism

Across systems, the same question:

How does variation among individuals drive population-level outcomes?

. . .

  • Seed dispersal: Some birds move farther → rare long-distance events shape forest structure
  • Disease transmission: Some hosts infect more → superspreaders drive outbreak dynamics
  • Metacommunities: Species co-occurrence patterns emerge from environmental filtering and spatial processes

. . .

Rudolph & Leibold (2020) Ecology

The computational literacy connection:

I study systems where individual-level mechanisms produce emergent patterns that are impossible to intuit without simulation.

The computational tools I develop for my research become the teaching materials I use to build capacity in others.

My research centers on individual variation as a mechanistic driver. Across seed dispersal, disease transmission, and metacommunity ecology, the unifying thread is that population-level outcomes are not predictable from species-level averages alone. You need to account for variation among individuals.

This is also where computational literacy becomes essential: these are systems where simulation is not just a convenience, it is the only way to understand what’s happening. You cannot derive the distribution of seed shadows analytically when frugivores differ in movement. You cannot predict outbreak probability from R₀ alone when transmission is heterogeneous. The computational implementation is the scientific reasoning.

And because I am building these tools for my own research, I am positioned to teach others how to build their own tools for their own questions.

Another example: Internal structure of metacommunities

The question: When do species co-occurrence patterns reflect environmental filtering vs. spatial processes?

The computational challenge: Joint Species Distribution Models (jSDMs) are black boxes unless you understand: - What they assume about species interactions - What they estimate about environmental responses - When residual correlations mean something biological vs. statistical artifact

Rudolph & Leibold (2020) Ecology

This is a computational literacy problem:

Students can run sjSDM() and get output. But can they: - Interpret what the latent factors represent? - Diagnose when the model is overfit? - Translate between the residual correlation matrix and macroecological patterns?

The code runs. The understanding requires moving between the ecological question, the statistical structure, and the computational implementation.

We teach this: Advanced ecology course co-taught with Mathew Leibold using National Lakes Assessment data across 9 ecoregions. Students fit sjSDMs and characterize macroecological patterns (species-area relationships, relative abundance distributions, Taylor’s power law) to ask: when do scaling laws hold, and when do they break?

The metacommunity work with Mathew Leibold is another case study in computational literacy. Joint Species Distribution Models have become standard tools in community ecology, but they are computationally intensive and statistically complex. Students learn to run them without understanding what they are actually estimating or when to trust the output.

We co-teach an advanced graduate ecology course where students work with National Lakes Assessment data (over 9,000 lakes across 9 ecoregions), fit jSDMs to understand how environmental filtering and spatial processes shape freshwater community composition across phytoplankton, zooplankton, and benthic macroinvertebrates, and connect those process-level results to pattern-level macroecological scaling laws.

The final project is structured as a symposium: each student takes a pair of ecoregions, characterizes macroecological patterns, fits sjSDMs, and asks what the models reveal about when and why scaling laws hold or break down. It is computational literacy in practice: build it, run it, break it, interpret it.

This is exactly the same challenge as the EVT/disease work. The biological question requires simulation and statistical inference. The tools exist. The gap is in the conceptual understanding that lets you use the tools correctly and interpret the results appropriately.

The origin: Collared aracari & seed dispersal

  • Primary dispersers of Virola trees in Costa Rican rainforest
  • Most seeds land close to parent — but rare long-distance events are not that rare when we account for individual variation in movement
  • Establishing the link between movement and long-distance dispersal

Holbrook 2011 Biotropica

My dissertation. These birds are the primary dispersal vector for canopy trees in tropical forests. The biological question: where do seeds go? The statistical question: how do you characterize a rare long-distance event that you may have only seen once or twice?

This is a computational literacy problem in disguise. The data are sparse GPS tracks. The model is a movement simulation. The inference target is the tail of the dispersal kernel. None of this is accessible through textbook statistics alone.

Averages hide the story

  • Bolnick and colleagues looked across 93 species and found that individual specialization is widespread across taxa — different individuals are doing different things
  • Focusing on variation among individuals, not species averages, reveals the mechanisms behind seed dispersal outcomes.

Bolnick et al. The American Naturalist 2003

Zwolak. Biological Reviews 2018

Individual variation in seed dispersers — personality, age, sex, specialization — shapes where seeds go and whether they survive. The link between individual variation and seed fate runs almost entirely through movement ecology. Focusing on variation among individuals, not species averages, reveals the mechanisms behind seed dispersal outcomes. Convex relationship: a population with variation disperses more seeds than predicted from the mean. Concave relationship: a population with variation disperses fewer seeds than predicted from the mean.

Here is what individual variation looks like. Each aracari has a different movement pattern. When you pool them into a single kernel, you lose the variation that drives rare long-distance events. When you account for individual differences, the probability of long-distance dispersal increases substantially.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT) & Extreme Value Theory (EVT)

The special rules in statistics

  • CLT = gather data → take average → repeat many times
    • averages are normally distributed

The Central Limit Theorem states that the distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution as the sample size increases, regardless of the population’s original distribution.

Extreme Value Theory states that regardless of the underlying data-generating process, the behavior of extreme observations — the rare, large events in the tail — converges to a Generalized Extreme Value distribution. The tail has its own universal structure.

The GEV distribution (shape ξ, scale σ, location μ) governs the tail. When ξ > 0, the tail is heavy (Fréchet domain). Rare events are rarer than normal, but when they happen, they are extreme.

The CLT is the familiar story — sample means converge to normal. EVT is the less familiar but equally fundamental story — extreme observations converge to a different universal family. The shape parameter ξ tells you what kind of tail you have. When ξ > 0, you are in the Fréchet domain: heavy tail, fat tail, the kind of tail that produces seed dispersal to 1 kilometer or superspreading events.

This is not an esoteric statistical technique. This is a foundational result about how rare events behave. And in ecology, rare events often matter disproportionately.

EVT in action: Individual variation drives long-distance dispersal

Simulation strategy:

  1. Fit individual movement models to GPS data
  2. Simulate seed dispersal under two scenarios:
    • Pooled: all birds drawn from a single movement distribution
    • Individual: each bird’s movement drawn from its own distribution
  3. Fit a Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD) to the tail (distances > 90th percentile)

Result: When I allow individual variation in frugivore movement, I get more long-distance dispersal events in my simulated seed shadows.

The shape parameter ξ > 0 (fat tail), and the conditional probability of a seed reaching beyond 500m or 1km is meaningfully higher under individual variation than under the pooled model.

This is the computational literacy payoff. The biological question — does individual variation matter for long-distance dispersal — requires simulation to answer. The statistical framework — EVT and GPD fits to the tail — gives you the tools to quantify it. The computational implementation — individual-based movement models parameterized from telemetry data — is the bridge.

Individual variation in disease transmission

“Population-level analyses often use average quantities to describe heterogeneous systems…”
— Lloyd-Smith et al. Nature 2005

Sound familiar?

\(R_0\) is an average. It hides the same individual variation we saw in dispersal kernels:

  • Most individuals infect zero or one
  • A few infect many — superspreaders

Individual reproductive number, \(\nu\), as a random variable representing the expected number of secondary cases caused by a particular infected individual.

  • Same \(R_0\), six different epidemiological worlds.
  • More heterogeneity means more outbreaks die out, but the ones that don’t are explosive.
  • k describes the shape of that individual variation

The opening quote of Lloyd-Smith is the same argument I’ve been making about seed dispersal for the last five slides. Population averages hide the mechanism. R₀ hides individual variation in infectiousness exactly the way a pooled dispersal kernel hides individual variation in movement.

Lloyd-Smith’s solution was statistical — fit a distribution, estimate R₀ and k. That works when you have contact tracing data, which you have for SARS, measles, smallpox. You do not have that for wildlife pathogens. You have GPS collars.

The branching process is the entire model — there is no SIR, no compartments, no population dynamics. It is a purely probabilistic model of individual-level transmission heterogeneity.

The mechanistic foundation

Ponciano & Capistrán (2011) derive the incidence rate from first principles:

  • An infected individual has realized disease dispersion \(a\)
  • Number of successful transmission encounters \(X(a)\) follows a pure birth process
  • Probability of at least one infection:

\[P(X(a) \geq 1) = 1 - e^{-a \cdot b \cdot h(I)}\]

When dispersion effort \(\Lambda\) is exponentially distributed across individuals:

\[P(X(a) \geq 1) = \int_0^\infty \left(1 - e^{-\lambda h(I)}\right) \alpha e^{-\alpha \lambda} \, d\lambda = \frac{h(I)}{h(I) + \alpha}\]

Ponciano & Capistrán. PLoS Computational Biology 2011

Individual variation matters here:
The exponential distribution for \(\Lambda\) already allows individuals to differ in how much they disperse pathogen.
But the exponential has a thin tail — it does not generate superspreaders.

What if dispersion effort follows a heavier-tailed distribution?
What if — like seed dispersers — some infected individuals are true long-distance movers?

Ponciano and Capistrán gave us the scaffolding. The pure birth process is elegant: the probability that you produce at least one infection grows with your realized dispersion effort a. The exponential model for Lambda averages over individual heterogeneity and gives you the Lotka-Haldane-Dobzhansky incidence rate. But the exponential is thin-tailed — it cannot generate the kind of extreme dispersion events that produce superspreaders. We need to go one step further.

From movement to superspreaders: The Lomax emerges

What if dispersion effort \(a\) is itself heterogeneous?

Allow dispersion effort to be distributed as an exponential distribution, whose parameter is sampled from a gamma distribution:

Let \(A \sim \text{Gamma}(\theta, \tau)\) — individual variation in transmission potential — and \(Y \mid A \sim \text{Exp}(A)\) — realized dispersion given that potential.

Then the marginal distribution of \(Y\) is:

\[f_Y(y) = \int_0^\infty a e^{-ay} \cdot \frac{\theta^\tau}{\Gamma(\tau)} a^{\tau-1} e^{-\theta a} \, da = \frac{\tau \theta^\tau}{(y + \theta)^{\tau+1}}\]

This is the Lomax distribution — special case of a GPD, a heavy-tailed distribution with support on \([0, \infty)\).
The tail emerges mechanistically from the compound structure.

And the probability of transmission under Lomax-distributed dispersion:

\[P(X(a) \geq 1) = \int_0^\infty \left(1 - e^{-abE}\right) \frac{\tau \theta^\tau}{(a+\theta)^{\tau+1}} \, da\]

The Gamma is the movement

\(\theta\) and \(\tau\) are not abstract parameters — they characterize how individuals in a population move. Some animals are homebodies, some are explorers.

That variation across individuals, captured by the Gamma, is what drives the heavy tail.

Allow the movement to vary, and EVT drops out the other end.

Rudolph & Ponciano (2025) bioRxiv | Rudolph (in prep)

Ponciano-Capistrán give you a first-principles framework where transmission probability depends on a dispersion effort parameter a, and they model the heterogeneity in a across individuals using an exponential distribution.

What if we let that exponential rate itself vary? Instead of a fixed rate, you draw it from a Gamma distribution — and that Gamma is the movement. The parameters θ and τ characterize how individuals in a population move. The Gamma is biologically interpretable as the movement kernel.

When you integrate out that Gamma-distributed rate, the marginal distribution of dispersion effort is Lomax. And the Lomax is a special case of the Generalized Pareto — which is exactly the EVT family.

This is a natural extension of an existing framework, and when you make this one biologically motivated move, EVT drops out the other end.

Simulation

This Shiny app lets you explore the parameter space. You can adjust θ and τ and see how the shape parameter ξ changes, how the tail behavior changes, how the probability of extreme events changes. This is computational literacy in action: the interactive tool is not just a demonstration, it is a reasoning environment.

Where the framework goes: American mink in Chile

Invasive semiaquatic carnivore in Chilean Patagonia

  • Disperses along river networks
  • Documented hosts: canine distemper, parvovirus, Mycobacterium bovis
  • Potential bridge host to native species: coypu, pudú, otters

Santibañez et al. (2025). Frontiers in Veterinary Science
Hernandez et al. (2024) Acta Tropica

How can we use mink movement along river corridors to predict pathogen spread potential and spillover risk to native species?

Lack of data and time asks for a simulation framework

Crego et al. (2018). PLoS One

American mink in Chilean waterways — invasive, semiaquatic, dispersing along river networks. This is a tractable one-dimensional dispersal problem. They carry pathogens and are positioned to act as bridge hosts. The question is whether the tail of their movement kernel predicts pathogen spread potential before outbreak data exist.

This is where the EVT framework pays off. We can parameterize the tail from sparse telemetry data using Peaks Over Threshold methods. We can link that tail to the Lomax dispersion effort framework. And we can simulate outbreak dynamics on river networks to ask: what is the probability of a pathogen reaching a native species population 50 km downstream? What is the probability that a single introduced mink sparks an outbreak?

These are not questions you can answer with average dispersal distances. These are questions about extreme events. And EVT gives you the tools to formalize them.

This is the power of EVT:

You don’t need to observe a 100 km dispersal event to estimate the probability of a 100 km dispersal event.

You fit the tail from the data you have, and the theory tells you what the tail predicts about events you haven’t seen.

This is also the power of computational literacy:

The simulation framework is not just analysis. It is scenario exploration. It is a reasoning tool for asking “what if” questions before the system tells you the answer.

This is where the research and the computational literacy mission converge. I am building a movement simulator for mink because I need it for my research on disease spread in Patagonia. But the simulator is also a teaching tool. The EVT parameterization from telemetry data is a methodological contribution, but it is also a case study in how to formalize rare event prediction from sparse data.

When I teach workshops on simulation in disease ecology, I use this mink system as the example. When I develop educational materials on EVT, I use the seed dispersal and disease transmission examples side by side to show how the same statistical framework applies across systems.

The research generates the materials. The materials build capacity. The capacity creates new collaborations and new research questions.
